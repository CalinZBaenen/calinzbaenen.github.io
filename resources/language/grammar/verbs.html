What is a verb?

A verb is a word that describes an action that will happen, is happening, or has happened.

Verbs have two major defining attributes, transitivity and copularity.
If the verb is transitive, there will be words proceeding it, called an object; sometimes, verbs take two objects, a direct object and an indirect object.

Here are some examples of verbs

  • Running
  • Jump
  • Walked
  • Broke
  • Turn
  • Shone

What is transitivity?

Transitivity is the quality of a verb taking one or multiple objects, which are typically nominals.
For example, the syntax of an intransitive verb is [noun (subject)] [verb], while the syntax of a transitive verb is [nominal (subject)] [verb] [nominal (indirect object)?] [nominal (direct object)].

Here are some examples of sentences using transitive verbs:

  • She gave him a book.
  • I am playing a game.
  • They found a cave.
  • He bought a new TV.
  • You won the race.
  • We scared the birds.

Here are some examples of sentences using intransitive verbs:

  • She is smiling.
  • I am running.
  • They are exploring.
  • He is laughing.
  • You are dancing.
  • We are strolling through the park.

Direct Object vs Indirect Object

The direct object of a verb is the object that is inherently affected by the verb.
For example, compare "She gave him a book." and "She gave away a book.". In both, "a book" is the direct object because it is the thing given away, it is effected by the giving. In contrast, "him" is the indirect object because the person being referred to isn't necessarily affected by being given the book.

What is copularity?

When a verb is copular, instead of signifying an action, it signifies that whatever proceeds the verb is a way to qualify or identify the subject.
For example, in the sentence "The house is big.", "is" is the copular verb, and "big" is a quality "the house".
